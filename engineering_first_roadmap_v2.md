# 1. Executive Summary

The highest-probability route to a $5M–$10M net worth is not full-time independent trading. It's building a rare, highly compensated engineering profile, moving into roles with increasing ownership, investing aggressively, and getting real exposure to equity or business upside.

```text
Software Engineer
    ↓
ML Systems / AI Infrastructure Engineer
    ↓
Senior Engineer / Technical Lead
    ↓
Staff / Principal Engineer / Engineering Manager
    ↓
Founding Engineer / Startup Leadership / AI Infra Leadership
```

Your primary specialization: **ML Systems and AI Infrastructure Engineering** — this draws on your actual strengths (model serving, backend APIs, distributed systems, Kafka streaming, evaluation systems, technical leadership).

Your secondary specialization: **Distributed Systems and Platform Engineering**.

**Be honest about the ceiling.** The $2M–$4M range is achievable through a strong engineering career and disciplined investing alone. The $6M–$10M range realistically requires staff/principal scope _plus_ meaningful equity or an unusually strong company outcome — not base compensation. Treat the upper end of the target as upside to build toward, not a default outcome to plan around.

- **Above $10M:** usually requires meaningful ownership, not salary alone.

The goal is to become technically rare, economically valuable, and increasingly exposed to ownership — not just employable.

## 6.3 Flagship project: rebuild, don't reuse

At VSF you led five engineers building a modular RAG system (ingestion → retrieval → reranking → generation → review UI) with Pydantic contracts across services and a refusal path for insufficient evidence. That's real, production-grade experience — but you owned coordination and parts of it, not the whole stack, and the code/data are company IP you can't reuse.

**The project:** build your own end-to-end RAG pipeline from scratch, on public data, specifically to go deep on the parts you didn't personally build at VSF — likely the retrieval layer (BM25 + dense + multi-hop GraphRAG-style retrieval) and the refusal/evaluation logic. This is a stronger story than a tutorial clone or reused work: _"I led a team shipping this at VSF, then rebuilt it independently to own every layer."_

Depth targets:

- Own the full retrieval stack (hybrid BM25 + dense + multi-hop).
- Real evaluation harness (not just eyeballing outputs) — this plays directly to your DocIngestionBench experience.
- Load testing, latency benchmarks, and a documented failure-mode analysis.
- A short technical report explaining architecture, tradeoffs, and what changed from the VSF version.

## 6.4 Second, smaller build: RL sidecar policy

A focused build on the idea you're already exploring at VSF: a lightweight sidecar policy for the retrieve-again/answer/refuse decision, trained via RL without touching the underlying LLM. Keep this deliberately smaller in scope than the flagship — it's meant to demonstrate a specific, harder skill (RL fundamentals, policy training, decision-boundary evaluation), not to be a second full system.

If a UMass course allows a custom project, aim to make the course project _be_ this build rather than a separate parallel workload.
